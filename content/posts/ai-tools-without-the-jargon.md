Somewhere between the breathless headlines about AI changing everything and the dense technical explainers about transformers and tokens, there's a practical question that most people don't have a clear answer to: what can I actually do with this, and how do I start?

This post is for that question.

You don't need a computer science background to use AI tools effectively. You don't need to understand how they work any more than you need to understand combustion engines to drive a car. What helps is knowing what these tools are genuinely good for, where they tend to fail, and how to have realistic expectations.

## What's Available Right Now

The most widely used AI tools are available in your browser today, most of them for free. ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini all have free tiers. Microsoft's Copilot is built into Windows and Office. Google has integrated AI features into search and its Workspace tools. If you have a smartphone or a laptop, you already have access to the same tools people are writing about constantly.

You don't need to download anything or configure anything. Open a browser, create an account, and you can start immediately.

## What AI Is Actually Good For

The best mental model for AI tools is that they're exceptionally capable at working with text — generating it, summarizing it, transforming it, and explaining it. That covers more ground than it might seem.

**Drafting things you're not sure how to write.** A complaint letter to a landlord. A professional email when you're not sure about the tone. A cover letter for a job. You explain the situation in plain language, and the AI produces a draft you can refine. You still decide what to say; you just don't start from a blank page.

**Explaining complicated things in plain language.** Medical paperwork. A legal document. A technical article you wanted to understand but couldn't get through. Paste the text and ask the AI to explain it simply. This is one of the most genuinely democratizing things these tools do — knowledge that used to require expensive professionals to interpret is now accessible to anyone willing to ask.

**Summarizing.** Long reports, articles, terms of service, meeting notes. If you need the gist of something without reading all of it, AI is fast and usually accurate at pulling out the key points.

**Brainstorming.** Gift ideas for a specific person, meal plans for the week, names for a small business, ideas for a school project. AI is tireless and will generate options quickly. You still need to evaluate them — it won't know your brother hates sports merchandise — but having a long list to react to is often more useful than starting from nothing.

**Editing your own writing.** Not just grammar and spelling, but clarity. Ask it to "make this clearer" or "shorten this" or "make this less formal." It won't know your voice perfectly, but it can give you a version to compare against.

## Where AI Gets It Wrong

This part matters as much as the list of capabilities, and it gets less attention.

**AI makes things up.** This is the most important limitation to internalize. Language models generate plausible text — they don't retrieve verified facts. They will confidently state incorrect information, cite sources that don't exist, and produce statistics that sound reasonable but are fabricated. The technical term is "hallucination," but the practical implication is simple: never trust AI output for anything factual without verifying it independently. Use it for drafting and explaining; look things up yourself for facts.

**AI doesn't know what's happening right now.** Most language models are trained on data up to a cutoff date. Unless a tool explicitly has web search enabled, it doesn't know about recent news, current prices, who won last week's game, or anything that happened recently. Ask it about current events without search, and you'll often get information that's months or years out of date, stated with the same confidence as everything else.

**AI doesn't act in the world.** Asking it to send an email for you, book an appointment, or make a purchase won't work unless you're using a tool specifically designed for those tasks (and even then, treat the results carefully). AI tools respond to prompts — they don't have agency to take independent action.

**Privacy is a real consideration.** The text you send to these tools is processed by the companies behind them. Don't share sensitive personal information, financial details, medical specifics about real people, or anything you'd be uncomfortable having stored on someone else's server. Most tools have privacy policies that describe how they handle this; they're worth reading.

## How to Start

The most useful thing you can do right now is open one of these tools and try something concrete. Not as an experiment, but as a real task you actually need to do.

Start with something low-stakes: draft that email you've been putting off, ask it to explain a document you didn't understand, or just ask it a question you've been curious about. Interact with it like a knowledgeable but fallible assistant: give it clear context, check anything factual it tells you, and treat the output as a starting point, not a final answer.

The learning curve is mostly about developing a sense for what it's good at and where to be skeptical. That sense comes from use, not from reading about it.

There's no credential required. No background check. The same tool a software engineer uses to debug code is the same tool you can use to draft a letter or understand a medical bill. That part of it — the access — is already there.

What you do with it is up to you.
